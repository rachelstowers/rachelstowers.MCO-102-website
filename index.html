History of Asbestos Mining in Wittenoom, Western Australia


Wittenoom, Western Australia is known for its booming blue asbestos mining and milling operations that drew thousands of workers and residents to the area from the early-1940s to the mid-1960s. Blue asbestos is one of the most toxic types of asbestos, and as evidence of the health dangers of asbestos became more widespread, market demand for the material declined. At the end of 1966, Wittenoom mining operations shutdown just as quickly as they had begun, leaving behind a trail of destruction. This documentary reveals how the town of Wittenoom looks today, more than 50 years after mining closed. Devastating effects remain evident, including:
  • The displacement of thousands of Wittenoom residents
  • High rates of health problems caused by asbestos exposure in the region, such as mesothelioma
  • Contamination of water and land in Wittenoom and surrounding areas

1917-1939

Asbestos in Western Australia

The discovery of blue asbestos (Crocidolite) in Western Australia was recorded in 1917 by the Western Australian Mines Department (Bennett, n.d.). In 1939, Lang Hancock and his partner, Peter Wright, established an asbestos crushing plant in Wittenoom (Asbestos Diseases Society of Australia, 2018).
